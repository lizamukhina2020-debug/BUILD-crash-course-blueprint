The wave is bigger than the dot-com boom — and it’s still early

Quick context: Dot-com is the era when the consumer Internet went mainstream — the wave where Facebook, Google, Amazon, Spotify, Netflix, and countless startups turned browsers and apps into how billions live, work, and connect.

AI is the next chapter after that internet explosion. It isn’t a sidebar trend — it’s a new layer on how software gets built and who gets to build it. The people who start now — messy, curious, in public — will shape products, habits, and culture for years.

You don’t need a CS degree or a “technical cofounder” badge. Ordinary people can ship real things with tools + AI + persistence. The window is open; the ones who grab it get to change what exists in the world, not only consume it.

04 The idea

Answer in normal words:

  • Who is it for?
  • What is one clear thing it helps them do?
  • Why you — vs doing nothing or using something else?

Rough rule from the whiteboard: the bigger the pain and the more people who feel it, the more room you have for product–market fit — i.e. the more likely something can work if you execute. You’re not hunting “a small annoyance nobody shares.” You’re looking for a real problem with real humans.

Look for pain you actually live. If the idea is tied to something personal — a frustration you hit in daily life — you start with domain expertise without trying: you notice details outsiders miss, and you care enough to stay in the game when it gets boring. Scan your week for “this should not be this annoying.”

Reality check: version one can be embarrassing. That’s how you earn version five.

05 Cursor = your main guide

You don’t need to know how to code — at all.

Cursor is two things at once: a guide for your app (what to build, in what order, what to watch for) and the builder that turns plain language into real code. You say what you want — “add a sign-in screen,” “fix this crash” — it writes and edits the files. You steer; it types. Ask it to explain like you’re new. Ask what to click.

When I say “talk to Cursor,” that’s literally how SeedMind moved from messy idea to something in the App Store: questions, diffs, fixes, repeat.

★ Top tips (read this twice)

  1. Keep build conversations in Cursor. Don’t run the same project in parallel with ChatGPT “coding coach” threads — split context wasted my time and credits and slowed me down hard. One lane for the codebase.
  2. Paste screenshots. Error messages, confusing UI, something from another website, a concept you don’t get — snap it and drop it into Ask or Agent. A picture beats a long vague description.
  3. Use Composer 2 for your default Agent / Ask flow when you can — for me it’s been roughly an order of magnitude cheaper than alternatives I tried, and still very effective (Cursor changes pricing; always check their current page).
  4. Ask mode = questions, explanations, planning. Agent mode = bigger multi-file edits — always review the diff like a human tech lead.
  5. Credits & plans: you get usage on your plan; if you run out, you upgrade or turn on on-demand usage and set a limit / cap you’re comfortable with — then raise it if needed. (Exact options live in Cursor’s billing settings.)

Put AI in your app — the smart-money way

If you want a chat or agent in your product — like I did with SeedMind — every user message usually hits an API. That means tokens and real money for you as the builder. More usage = more bill — so your model choice matters.

After trying a lot of options, I recommend DeepSeek for most indie apps: quality that holds up next to big names, often at a fraction of the per-message cost versus routing everything through premium ChatGPT-class or flagship Gemini APIs. Same user experience idea — smarter spend on the backend.

API pricing changes constantly. Before you wire anything in, compare the official pages yourself — e.g. DeepSeek pricing, OpenAI, Google Gemini — and model your cost per active user.

07 Order that usually works

  1. Know your why. Be clear on why you’re building — that’s what you come back to when it gets hard.
  2. Outline the idea (bullets, not an essay): the problem you’re solving, who it’s for, and the one main outcome they get.
  3. Start in Cursor — new project, environment setup; it walks you through what to install.
  4. Ship small slices of product — one flow or feature after another until it’s enough to be useful, not perfect.
  5. Use it yourself, then put it in front of friends; watch what breaks or confuses people — that’s data, not shame.
  6. Ship when it’s stable enough for real use.
  7. Keep iterating — observe, fix, improve.

Magic question: “What’s the smallest next step?”
